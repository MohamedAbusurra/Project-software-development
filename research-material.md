# Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS)

## 1. What is IaaS?

Infrastructure as a Service, or IaaS, means renting the basic parts of computing instead of buying them. That covers compute power, storage, and networking, the stuff you'd normally get from owning your own physical servers. You request what you need over the internet and the provider takes care of the actual hardware.

IaaS is one of three cloud models, and it helps to see how they stack up. With IaaS you get the raw infrastructure and you handle everything built on top of it. PaaS (Platform as a Service) goes further and hands you the tools to build and run your applications. SaaS (Software as a Service) is the finished app you just sign in and use, like Gmail or Google Docs (Emma, 2025). Another way to picture it: IaaS is like renting an empty plot of land with the power and water already hooked up. SaaS is the opposite end, a house that's already built and ready to move into. PaaS sits somewhere in the middle.

## 2. The Origin: How AWS Started It

Before 2006, putting software online meant buying your own servers. You'd purchase the machines, set them up in a data center, and guess how much capacity you'd need ahead of time. Guess too low and your site crashed when traffic spiked. Guess too high and you paid for hardware that mostly sat there doing nothing.

Amazon Web Services changed this in 2006 when it launched S3 and EC2. S3 (Simple Storage Service) handled storage and EC2 (Elastic Compute Cloud) handled compute, and together they basically kicked off the cloud computing market we use today (Emma, 2025). Those two are the original IaaS products. After they came out, you could rent compute and storage by the hour, scale up when you got busy, and shut things off when you didn't. Infrastructure started working more like a utility bill than a big one-time purchase.

## 3. The Business Idea: CAPEX to OPEX

The reason this mattered so much comes down to money. Buying servers is a capital expense, or CAPEX, which is a big chunk of cash spent up front before the business has earned anything back. IaaS turned that into an operating expense, or OPEX, where you only pay for what you actually use each month (ResearchAndMarkets, 2026).

This is what people mean when they say the cloud removed the barrier between an idea and a global product. You didn't need a huge hardware budget just to test something out anymore. A small team could launch with very little and only spend more once real users started showing up. The upfront risk that used to kill a lot of projects before they even started was mostly gone.

## 4. How Big IaaS Is Now

Fast forward to now and this rental model is huge. The global IaaS market was around $175 billion in 2025 and is expected to hit roughly $197.25 billion in 2026, growing at about 12.7% a year (The Business Research Company, 2026). If it keeps that pace, it's projected to reach around $328.89 billion by 2030.

That growth is why IaaS counts as a current topic and not just tech history. It's been around for almost twenty years and it's still growing at double digits.

## 5. AWS's Market Position

AWS is still the biggest player in the market it helped create. It holds about 33% of cloud infrastructure spending and pulls in roughly $130 billion a year (BusinessTats, 2026). Microsoft Azure comes next at around 23%, and Google Cloud is third at about 12% (CloudZero, 2026). Add those three together and the Big Three control close to 68% of the whole market.

One thing worth flagging on the numbers: you'll sometimes see a 98% figure thrown around. That one comes from a website-tracking stat, not actual cloud spending, so I went with the 33% number because it reflects real market share.

## 6. The Next Chapter: IaaS Powers the AI Boom

What's driving cloud growth now is different from the early days. Back then it was mostly companies moving their existing apps off their own servers and onto the cloud. These days a big part of the growth is AI, specifically the heavy computing it takes to train and run AI models (BusinessTats, 2026).

So in a way it comes full circle. IaaS started in 2006 as a way to rent a server instead of buying one, and almost twenty years later that same setup is what the AI boom runs on. Every AI model that gets trained and every chatbot answering questions is using compute that someone is renting, which is the same basic idea AWS launched with EC2.
